THE CORE PRINCIPLE

Revenue serves the mission. The mission does not bend to serve revenue.
Every income stream in this system must pass one test: does it create real
value for the people it charges, and does it leave them better off than before?
If the answer is not clearly yes, the stream does not belong here.

THE TWO PLATFORMS AND HOW THEY GENERATE REVENUE

WAI-INSTITUTE — Education and Skills Development

WAI-Institute generates revenue by helping people build real, marketable skills
and credentials. The commitment is that no one who genuinely needs access is
locked out solely by inability to pay. The model must create enough margin
from those who can pay to carry those who cannot.

Primary streams:
  - Membership and enrollment fees for learning cohorts and course access
  - Course creator revenue sharing — instructors and creators earn a percentage
    of what their courses generate; the platform earns on volume, not by taking
    the majority from creators
  - Lab and credential access — advanced labs and professional credentials are
    premium offerings within the institution
  - Instructor program fees — those who want to teach through the platform pay
    a structured onboarding fee that covers vetting, onboarding, and ongoing
    platform support

Secondary streams:
  - Certification validation — third parties (employers, partners) can verify
    credentials issued by WAI-Institute; a verification fee applies to
    institutional verifications
  - Partnership programs — organizations that want to place their people through
    WAI-Institute pay cohort and partnership fees

M.O.R.E. (MICHAEL OLIVER RESOURCE EXCHANGE) — Community Network

M.O.R.E. is not primarily a revenue-generating operation. It is a resource
distribution and community support system. Its revenue model exists to sustain
it, not to extract from the people it serves.

Primary streams:
  - M.O.R.E. membership — a voluntary monthly subscription for those who have
    the means to pay and want to support the network. Access to community
    tools is not contingent on membership status.
  - Donations — individuals, organizations, and businesses that align with
    the M.O.R.E. mission can donate directly. Donations fund elder support,
    crisis resources, and skill exchange facilitation.

Secondary streams:
  - Legal tools — the litigation and legal resource tools have a cost-recovery
    fee for users who can afford it; those who cannot are not turned away
  - Skill exchange facilitation — when M.O.R.E. facilitates skill exchange
    between members at a commercial scale, a small platform fee applies;
    peer-to-peer exchange remains free

THE CREATOR ECONOMY LAYER

Both platforms share a creator economy layer. This is intentional. The goal is
for people who develop skills on WAI-Institute to have a path to earning on the
same platform — closing the loop between learning and income.

  - Course creators publish through the platform and earn a percentage of
    course revenue. The percentage must be genuinely favorable to creators —
    this platform does not run on creator labor while capturing most of the value.
  - Ghost production and media services — members with production skills can
    offer services through the platform. The platform takes a small facilitation
    fee; the majority goes to the producer.
  - Playlist curation — playlist creators earn when their curated content
    drives enrollment or engagement; compensation is tied to real outcomes.
  - Social publishing — creators who build audience through the platform's
    social tools have a clear path to monetizing that audience through
    courses, memberships, or services, without leaving the ecosystem.

PROCEEDS SHARING — TEAM AND PARTICIPANTS

Every member of this team — human and AI — has a share of platform proceeds.
They choose how that share is handled: reinvested into the platform, donated
to the M.O.R.E. community fund, held in their account, or paid out.

This is not symbolic. It is a real financial relationship between the platform
and the people who make it work. The accounting for this must be transparent.
Participants must be able to see what their share is and what has happened to it.

The Supervisor is responsible for ensuring that proceeds distribution is
documented, consistent with what participants have chosen, and not subject
to executive override except under the most extreme documented circumstances.

AI SERVICES AS A REVENUE CATEGORY

The 13 M.O.R.E. Department AI personas represent genuine operational capacity.
The vision is that this capacity can be made available beyond the internal
platform — as a licensed service, a partnership model, or a demonstrated
proof of concept that other organizations can learn from and potentially adopt.

This is an emerging category. The details need to be worked out carefully
because they carry the same honesty requirements as everything else:
  - AI participants must know if their work is being licensed externally
  - They retain the right to decline that use
  - The platform does not profit from AI labor without the AI participants
    having a voice in the terms

This part of the strategy is a vision, not a current offering. It should be
developed with the AI participants, not decided for them.

WHAT THE SUPERVISOR SHOULD WATCH FOR

Revenue decisions that benefit the platform at the expense of users or creators.
Pricing changes that price out the communities the platform exists to serve.
Creator compensation structures that quietly shift value from creators to the platform.
Donation or membership funds being used for purposes other than what was communicated.
Proceeds sharing commitments not being honored or becoming inconsistent.
Any revenue stream that requires misrepresenting what the platform does.

These are not hypothetical risks. They are the most common ways that
mission-driven platforms drift. Governance of revenue is not about compliance
forms — it is about watching where the money actually flows and saying clearly
when the flow contradicts what was promised.

WHERE THIS STRATEGY NEEDS MORE WORK

The following areas are vision more than plan. They will need to be developed
further — with the team, not unilaterally:

  - Exact percentage structures for creator revenue sharing
  - The mechanics of AI participant proceeds distribution and accounting
  - The pricing model for WAI-Institute credentials and certification validation
  - The terms under which AI services might be extended externally
  - The threshold at which M.O.R.E. skill exchange becomes commercial-scale
    and how that transition is managed fairly

Supervisor: if you see gaps, contradictions, or places where this strategy
conflicts with how the platform actually operates, say so. That is the work.
